Hero Pools and Their Impact on the Meta

Starting in week 5, Overwatch introduced hero pools into the game in hopes of keeping the competitive meta as fluid as possible. Each week, a different set of heroes are banned from play: 2 DPS, 1 tank and 1 support. Here, we’ll take a look at the meta before hero bans came into effect and what it has looked like each week since. A couple things to note:

  1. Just because a composition is here doesn’t mean it’s the only compositions played in a specific week. OWL teams like to experiment, and these comps are just the ones that teams liked to play the most.
  2. Every hero in Overwatch has such a unique playstyle and ability set and I can’t possibly do them any justice in a single paragraph. So, click on a hero’s portrait to get more detailed information on their abilities and general strategies.

Weeks 1-4 (No hero bans)


The first four weeks of the Overwatch League saw a very static meta based around Mei. Her ice wall is one of the most powerful abilities in the game due to the fact that she can block people off from their teammates and kill them solo. Her Blizzard can single-handedly win fights if used correctly and her self-healing makes her difficult to kill. Around her, newly-buffed Reinhardt and D.Va are the most mobile shields in the game, and Ana is the only support character that can handle the sheer amount of healing the tanks will need from long range, plus her Nano-Boost being a huge offensive advantage. The D.Va was usually the one to engage first due to her high mobility, and the main damage was done with the second damage hero, usually either a Cassidy or Widowmaker.


Most popular comp in weeks 1-4

Mei Cassidy Reinhardt D.Va Ana Lúcio

Week 5

Banned Heroes

Cassidy Widowmaker Reinhardt Moira

The first week of hero pools was a new experience for players, so many different comps were experimented with (like a comp simply replacing Cassidy with Reaper and Rein with Orisa). The comp that most teams decided to run by the end of the week was the quicker dive composition with Winston and D.Va. Usually the comp was run with Ana before Brigitte’s release, but Brigitte is better in dive due to her short-range passive healing, rather than the long-range sniper healing of Ana. Brig also acts as a counter to Tracer - the biggest damage dealer in this comp - so she is very useful when both teams are running the same composition. The second healer would be either Zenyatta for his damage or Lúcio for his fast movement speed. For damage, teams mostly chose Tracer and Soldier: 76 due to their fast movement speed, but also chose a variation with Sombra due to her ability hacking and EMP being highly effective on heroes like Zenyatta, D.Va and Winston.

Week 8

Banned Heroes

Sombra Soldier: 76 Winston Lúcio

Week 8 saw a rift in the league when it came to the meta. Since every player was sent home due to COVID-19 concerns and all matches were moved online, only teams in the same region they reside in (China, North America West + Seoul, North America East + Europe) will practice and play matches against each other. This means that China and NA developed their own styles of playing certain compositions. These differing comps, however, were only played about 50% of the time; the other half was played with an Orisa/Sigma double shield composition which was shared by all regions. Because Lúcio and Winston were banned, teams opted to go for a more stationary comp, which means playing the steadfast Orisa and Sigma. Their shields plus Baptiste’s immortality field combine to be a hard defense to crack. The Hanzo is used to deal consistent damage from afar, hopefully getting a one-shot kill so the team can fight with the man advantage.


Most popular comp in week 8 (double shield)

Hanzo Mei Orisa Sigma Zenyatta Baptiste

This double shield comp was the same in NA and China, but there were other comps that differed between the two regions. The Chinese teams opted to play to their strengths and play Pharah and Wrecking Ball in a dive-esque comp. Chengdu has the best Wrecking Ball in the League in Ameng, and Chengdu and Shanghai have the two best Pharahs in the league in JinMu and dding, so they knew they would get value out of these heroes. The Wrecking Ball would roll into the enemy team, pushing them back and creating space. The Pharah would then volley damage from above and a Widowmaker or Cassidy would get easy headshots after the Wrecking Ball’s disruption. Because of Pharah being so far up in the air, only a Mercy can fly up to her and keep her healed, so a hero that does a lot of healing like Ana is needed to help the other five players.


Week 8 dive comp (China)

Pharah Cassidy Wrecking Ball D.Va Ana Mercy

In North America, the second comp looks similar to the GOATS comp that dominated play last season. GOATS consisted of three tanks (Reinhardt, D.Va, Zarya) and three support heroes (Brigitte, Zenyatta, Lúcio), which is impossible now due to the implemented 2-2-2 role lock. Rein, Zarya and Brig stay from GOATS, with Doomfist, Reaper and Ana rounding out the six. The purpose of GOATS was to sustain; the barriers and sustained healing makes for long, drawn-out fights. Reaper can heal himself by dealing damage and Doomfist can get shields in the same way, so the sustain is still there in this modified composition. The biggest Ultimate combo in this comp is the “Space Jam”, where Zarya’s Graviton Surge and Doomfist’s Meteor Strike is combined to do massive damage. The name of the game in this comp was keeping the healers safe and outlasting the other team while the damage heroes try to take out whoever is most vulnerable.


Week 8 GOATS-esque comp (North America, Seoul & Europe)

Reaper Doomfist Reinhardt Zarya Ana Brigitte

Week 9

Banned Heroes

Mei Cassidy Wrecking Ball Brigitte

Week 9 saw bans of two of the most hated heroes in the competitive Overwatch scene: Mei and Brigitte. Both of their playstyles allowed for slow, drawn out fights that are amazing for analysts but can become fairly dry for viewers to sit through. With them gone, teams opted to run a variation of the “Moira Deathball” composition that was a niche pick before Brigitte and role lock. This comp before role lock was composed of four tanks (Rein, Winston, Zarya, D.Va) and two supports (Moira and Lúcio). This comp would be effective because of the massive amount of healing that Moira could dish out when the rest of the team was grouped up close to her (hence the name “Deathball”). This comp could be seen as a predecessor to GOATS, and a variation of this can be seen in the week 9 comps. The biggest factors in this comp are Reaper and Moira due to Moira’s aforementioned healing and Reaper’s insane close-up damage. Combining his Death Blossom with Reinhardt’s Earthshatter creates the classic “spin to win” play, and Zarya’s bubbles help Reaper and Rein get in close and do huge damage without taking too much damage themselves. In China, Zarya and Sombra were played especially often, with Sombra’s hack giving her team a huge advantage if they could follow up the hack with a pickoff.


Week 9 deathball comp (China)

Reaper Sombra Reinhardt Zarya Moira Lúcio

North American Overwatch players have had a known disdain for Sombra since her recent buffs because of how annoying her hacks can get while playing. Thus, the NA and EU squads chose to play the one hero that can best ensure that Sombra could get as little value as possible: Torbjörn. His turret insta-locks onto Sombra and would notify the team of her position after she comes out of invisibility, so playing her into a Torb is a nightmare for her team. Torb’s Molten Core is particularly effective on maps with objectives to capture due to the space that it creates to fold on the enemy team. If teams didn’t feel comfortable playing Torb, they instead played Doomfist because of his high ability to pick off single enemies. NA teams also chose to play D.Va instead of Zarya, mainly due to her increased mobility and the strength of her defense matrix.


Week 9 deathball comp (North America, Seoul & Europe)

Reaper Torbjörn Reinhardt D.Va Moira Lúcio
